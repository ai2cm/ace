## Answers to your PR comments:

### Comment 1 (Finding 4): Volcanic events vs reanalysis artifacts?

**Mixed — some are artifacts, some are volcanic, and the original finding conflated them.**

**1986 specific_total_water_0 (-3.9σ): REANALYSIS ARTIFACT, not volcanic.**
- The weekly data shows the drop happens in a single week: March 30 → April 6, 1986, a sharp ~10% drop perfectly aligned with the **April 1, 1986 ERA5 parallel stream merge**.
- A volcanic signal from El Chichón (1982) would have appeared 1982-1984 and decayed gradually. A sharp drop 4 years later at an exact stream merge date is not volcanic.
- The monthly plot confirms: stable values through Jan-March 1986, then cliff-edge drop at April 1.

**1993 air_temperature_0 (-3.1σ) and air_temperature_1 (-2.5σ): MOSTLY VOLCANIC (Pinatubo).**
- Pinatubo erupted June 1991. The data shows clear stratospheric warming of ~0.9K in 1991-1992 (exactly the expected volcanic aerosol heating signature), then cooling as aerosol dissipated in 1993.
- The Sep 1992 ERA5 stream boundary shows NO sharp discontinuity in weekly temperature data — the transition is smooth.
- The warming→cooling pattern matches Pinatubo literature perfectly. There may be some minor stream boundary contribution superimposed, but the dominant signal is real volcanic physics.

**2000 specific_total_water_0 (-2.5σ): LIKELY ARTIFACT.**
- This aligns with a known Jan 2000 stream merge boundary.
- The spatial before/after plot shows a near-uniform global negative offset — characteristic of a systematic bias shift, not weather or volcanic signals.

**Should volcanic aerosol be a forcing?** The Pinatubo signal is real and large (~0.9K stratospheric warming). However, ACE is currently trained on 1940-2022 minus excluded periods, so volcanic events (El Chichón, Pinatubo, Agung 1963) are in the training data. If the model has no volcanic aerosol forcing, it has no way to predict these events and they just add noise. Two options:
1. Add stratospheric aerosol optical depth as a forcing (would let the model learn volcanic responses)
2. Exclude major volcanic periods from training (simpler, but loses data)

This is worth a discussion — it depends on whether you want ACE to handle volcanic scenarios.

### Comment 2 (Finding 5): Why does USWRFsfc have negative values?

**They are floating-point noise from ERA5 post-processing, not physically meaningful.**

Key evidence:
- The negative values are **absurdly small**: median = -0.00002 W/m², max magnitude = 0.06 W/m². Only 5 values (out of 95M) exceed -0.01 W/m².
- **99.3% of negatives occur at nighttime** (where DSWRFsfc = 0), so these are points where the true value is exactly zero.
- They're uniformly distributed spatially (slightly more at poles where there are more nighttime hours).
- DSWRFsfc and USWRFtoa use exact zeros for nighttime, but USWRFsfc apparently went through a different interpolation/processing step in the ERA5 pipeline that introduced tiny floating-point residuals instead of hard-clamping to zero.

**For training purposes**: These are negligible (6 orders of magnitude below the signal). They shouldn't affect model training. The "23% negative" statistic from the original finding is technically correct but misleading — it's really "23% are nighttime values that should be exactly zero but have ~10⁻⁵ W/m² noise."
